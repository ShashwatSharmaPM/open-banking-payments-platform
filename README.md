# Open Banking Payments Platform: Regulation to Revenue

> Building a PSD2 compliance platform from a regulatory whitepaper, through PoC, to a production product serving 7 global banking clients and generating $17M in ARR.

## Context

**Company type**: Global consulting and technology services firm (Fintech product arm)
**My role**: Started as Go-to-Market Consultant, transitioned to Product Manager
**Timeline**: 2017-2020 (3 years, full lifecycle from concept to mature product)

---

## The Problem

In 2018, PSD2 (Payment Services Directive 2) was reshaping the European banking landscape. The regulation mandated that banks open their payment infrastructure to third-party providers via APIs, effectively creating open banking.

For banks, this was simultaneously a compliance deadline and an existential question. They had to comply. But most banks, especially small and mid-sized ones, didn't have the internal capability to build PSD2-compliant API infrastructure from scratch.

Our hypothesis: if we could build a turnkey PSD2 enabler platform, banks could achieve compliance in dramatically shorter timelines than building in-house. One product, many banks, fast compliance.

---

## My Journey: GTM Strategist to Product Manager

### Phase 1: Market Validation and PoC

I joined as a go-to-market strategist. My initial job was to validate the market opportunity and help sell the concept.

I went deep into the PSD2 compliance documentation, understood the technical requirements (API standards, authentication protocols, third-party provider registration), and mapped the regulatory timeline against bank readiness.

We built a proof of concept and took it to two large banking prospects. The PoC demonstrated that our platform could handle the core compliance requirements: account information services, payment initiation services, and the security protocols mandated by the directive.

### Phase 2: PoC to Product (My Transition)

When the prospects showed serious interest, the question became: who owns the product build?

I made the case that I should. I'd gone through the compliance document multiple times, understood what we'd promised to the prospects, knew the regulatory nuances, and had direct relationships with the banking teams evaluating us. Bringing in a new PM would mean months of ramp-up on domain knowledge we couldn't afford to lose.

I got the opportunity. This was my entry into product management.

### Phase 3: Building and Scaling

**What I built:**
- The core PSD2 compliance engine handling account information and payment initiation APIs
- Multi-bank configuration: one platform, customizable per bank's existing infrastructure and regulatory jurisdiction
- Security and authentication layers aligned with PSD2's Strong Customer Authentication (SCA) requirements
- Integration interfaces with banks' existing core banking systems (each bank had different legacy infrastructure)

**How I scaled it:**
- Developed a 5-year product roadmap with 50+ revenue streams and 150+ features
- Analyzed product performance metrics to recalibrate infrastructure, achieving 64% reduction in operational costs
- Expanded from 2 initial banking clients to 7 global clients across multiple European jurisdictions
- Managed the tension between customization (each bank wanted unique configurations) and scalability (we needed to serve many banks from one platform)

---

## Key Decisions

**1. Platform vs. bespoke builds**
The temptation was to build custom solutions for each bank (consulting DNA of the parent organization). I pushed for a platform approach: one core product with configurable modules. This was harder upfront but meant each new client didn't require a ground-up build.

**2. Compliance as the floor, not the ceiling**
Banks initially only cared about regulatory compliance. But I saw that once the API infrastructure existed, it could be used for much more: new fintech partnerships, enhanced payment experiences, data analytics. The 5-year roadmap was built around this: comply first, then monetize the infrastructure.

**3. Operational cost optimization**
By analyzing performance metrics across deployments, I identified that certain infrastructure components were over-provisioned for smaller banks and under-provisioned for larger ones. Recalibrating this reduced operational costs by 64% without impacting SLA compliance.

## What I Learned

1. **Domain expertise is a PM superpower.** I didn't become PM because of PM skills. I became PM because I knew the regulatory landscape better than anyone else on the team. Deep domain knowledge let me make product decisions that a generalist PM would have needed months to understand.

2. **Regulations create markets.** PSD2 wasn't just a compliance burden. It was a forcing function that created demand for an entirely new product category. Timing the product to the regulatory deadline was as important as building the right features.

3. **The GTM-to-PM transition is underrated.** Understanding how the product is sold, who the buyer is (compliance officer vs. CTO vs. CEO), and what objections they raise made me a better PM. I built features that I knew would close deals, not just features that looked good in a demo.

4. **Platform thinking in a services organization is a constant negotiation.** A consulting firm's instinct is to customize for every client. Building a scalable platform required saying no to bespoke requests and showing that the platform approach would generate more revenue with less effort over time.
